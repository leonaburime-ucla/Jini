# Premium UI

Use this skill to raise perceived quality without sacrificing clarity, conversion, performance, accessibility, or maintainability.

## Workflow

1. Define the desired first-impression feeling: calm, confidence, authority, excitement, exclusivity, technical competence, or another specific mood.
2. Design for scanners first: make the offer, audience, proof, and next action obvious within seconds.
3. Reduce cognitive load: simplify navigation, remove weak copy, make each section serve one primary job, and preserve strong visual hierarchy.
4. Add premium signals deliberately: bespoke assets, cohesive brand system, real imagery, credible metrics, careful typography, generous spacing, and subtle interaction feedback.
5. Protect business outcomes: keep CTAs clear, page flow intentional, pricing/filtering honest where appropriate, and pages focused on the customer result.
6. Validate with a blink test, mobile performance check, responsive review, and conversion-path review.

## Standards

- Prefer clear, confident layouts over decorative complexity.
- Use real product, team, customer, venue, or process imagery when authenticity matters; avoid generic stock-photo signals.
- Make bespoke visuals purposeful: guide the eye, break up content, explain value, or create brand distinction.
- Keep animation subtle enough to be felt more than noticed. Use it for feedback, orientation, and flow, not spectacle.
- Let copy sell outcomes and transformations, not just features or internal history.
- Give every page a job and every section a next step.
- If a site is meant to be client-owned or maintained, design editing surfaces and content models that make updates practical.
- Avoid premium-looking decisions that harm load time, accessibility, comprehension, or buyer confidence.
